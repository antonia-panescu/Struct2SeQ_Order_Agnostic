## Faithful Shujun protocol on 240mer + symmetric rescue

Submission day. Spent the early hours fixing the AR-baseline experiment
that was silently broken since 2026-05-08, then ran the proper
apples-to-apples comparison Shujun's `test_240.py` defines.

### What was broken

Our `orig_struct2seq_l2r_240mer` baseline used `generate_sequence_batched(p=0.0)`
— pure argmax decoding under L→R. At K=1000 this looked like 14.4%
perfect / 5/20 puzzles solved, which coincidentally matched Shujun's
published 240mer Struct2SeQ numbers and so passed the smell test.

It was wrong:

- L→R + argmax is essentially deterministic (the model emits one
  position at a time in fixed order, no sampling). Across K=1000 we
  produced **only 340 unique sequences out of 20,032 samples** — the
  remaining 98% are exact duplicates from CUDA float non-determinism
  occasionally breaking near-tied argmaxes. So our K=1000 budget was
  wasted: effectively K~17.
- Shujun caught this in Slack ("wait if you just do argmax decoding
  with L2R wouldn't you always get the same design?").
- Our own bidir model under L→R + argmax shows the same effect:
  `bidir_identity_240mer` → 346 unique seqs across 20,032 samples.
- For our random-perm decoder, argmax-only is fine — random orders
  give 1000+ unique sequences from K=1000. **Order-randomization is
  the diversity engine.**

The published K=98,000 protocol from `test_240.py` is precisely the
workaround for AR's argmax-determinism: three different sampling
strategies inject token-level randomness so K=98,000 actually produces
98,000 different designs.

### Faithful 3-strategy implementation

Read `Struct2SeQ/test_240.py` lines 116-143 for the exact protocol:

```python
sequence1 = generate_sequence_batched(... p=0.05)        # eps-greedy uniform-among-allowed at 5%
sequence2 = generate_sequence_batched(... p=0.10)        # eps-greedy at 10%
sequence3 = generate_sequence_batched_sample(... p=1)    # Q-softmax sampling
# topk_sequence = generate_sequence_topk(...)            # COMMENTED OUT
```

Plus rescue post-hoc on the best non-perfect sequence (lines 177-225):
identify `diff_pos` via `cor2vec()` (positions where target's
pair-partner vec ≠ predicted's pair-partner vec); if `len(diff_pos) ≤ 4`
recursively enumerate all 4^k mutations via `search_v2.get_mutated()`;
score each, keep any improvement.

Implementation: `evaluation/run_rescue.py` imports `get_mutated` and
`cor2vec` directly from Shujun's `Struct2SeQ/search_v2.py` and
`Struct2SeQ/Functions.py` — no reimplementation.

We also extended `evaluation/run_ok7_eval.py` with a `--sampling-mode`
flag (`argmax | qsoftmax | epsilon`) and `--sampling-p` so we can run
the same 3 strategies on our bidir checkpoint.

Beam search (topk) was tested separately at k=50 and k=128 since it
OOMs on 240mer at the originally-requested k=333. It's *not* part of
the headline `test_240.py` protocol (commented out by Shujun).

### Why rescue helps AR but not us

Symmetric rescue — same algorithm, same diff_pos ≤ 4 threshold,
applied to the per-puzzle best non-perfect sequence:

- AR + rescue: 4/5 rescue attempts reached jaccard=1.0 (puzzles 0, 12,
  13, 17 went from near-miss to perfect).
- Ours + rescue: 0/6 rescue attempts reached jaccard=1.0 (all 6
  attempted puzzles got max_jaccard ≤ 0.989).

Mechanistic interpretation:

- AR explores ~17 unique paths from one entry point. Its best
  non-perfect is "almost the right design, off by 1-2 specific bases
  that, once corrected, snap the structure into place." Rescue's
  4^k enumeration lands on those specific bases.
- Our random-perm decoder explores 1000 unique paths across the global
  design space. The best non-perfect is the closest of 1000 broad
  attempts; failure modes are **structurally entrenched** (the
  surrounding context belongs to a different basin). Mutating 2-4
  positions doesn't fix the topology because the *whole sequence
  context* is wrong, not a few specific bases.

Verified by also building a *bidirectional* rescue (`run_bidir_rescue.py`)
that uses our in-painting capability instead of 4^k enumeration: take
the seed's best non-perfect, fix all non-diff_pos to that seed's
nucleotides, regenerate just the diff_pos with K=100 random-perm
samples conditioned on the full target structure. **Result: identical
max_jaccard per puzzle** (0.956, 0.979, 0.978, 0.981, 0.984, 0.989) —
our 100 model-guided samples produced the same ceiling as Shujun's 256
enumerated mutations. So local rescue space is already saturated by
both methods on these seeds; the failures are genuinely structural.

This is a complementary-capabilities finding for the paper:
- Rescue is a tool for AR's narrow exploration.
- Random-perm + argmax replaces *the need* for rescue by exploring
  broadly from the start.
- They are different paradigms, not different tools.

### Bidir-rescue: included as analysis, NOT a contribution

We don't claim "bidirectional rescue" as a method — it produces 0
improvements over plain `bidir_random`. We include it in the paper
only as evidence that the structural-ceiling explanation is correct
(both rescue paradigms hit the same ceiling).

### Why rescue is asymmetric — over-pair vs under-pair failure modes

After Shujun pushed back ("rescue should just work for any sequence,
it's independent of decoding order"), did manual verification on
puzzle 12 (UC2414, 240mer):

- AR's best non-perfect: diff_pos=[109,138] — predicted has them
  paired (A-U), target wants them unpaired. **Over-pairing failure.**
  Rescue: mutate either to break WC complementarity → pair drops →
  jaccard 1.0. ✓
- Ours' best non-perfect: diff_pos=[14,21] — target wants them
  paired, predicted has both unpaired. Bases there are already C-G
  (Watson-Crick). **Under-pairing failure.**
  Rescue: scored all 16 mutations manually. **None** of the 6 WC
  combinations (A-U, U-A, C-G, G-C, G-U, U-G) produces a predicted
  14↔21 pair. Max jaccard across 16 mutations = 0.984, no
  improvement. RibonanzaNet's pair prediction depends on long-range
  surrounding context (bases 1-13, 15-20, 22-203); local mutation
  at the diff_pos doesn't change that.

So rescue runs identically on any sequence, but its *success* depends
on the seed's failure-mode type. AR's narrow exploration + sampling
tends to produce over-pair errors (locally rescuable). Random-perm +
argmax tends to produce under-pair errors (not locally rescuable when
the missing pair is context-dependent).

Verified this is *systematic*, not a one-off bad seed: multi-seed
bidir-rescue from top-5 non-perfect seeds gives the same ceiling
(max jaccard 0.90-0.99 across all attempted seeds). Random-perm
argmax-only systematically produces under-pair failures on its
unsolved puzzles. Going to lower-jaccard seeds would only give worse
seeds in the same failure class.

Discussion-section framing: rescue is not architecture-blind. Its
effectiveness depends on failure-mode locality of the generator.
Random-perm decoding produces error modes that **expose**
RibonanzaNet's context-dependent pair-prediction bias, in a way
that L→R AR doesn't.

### Multi-seed bidir-rescue tested (still hits structural ceiling)

After single-seed bidir-rescue produced 0 improvements over plain
`bidir_random`, ran a multi-seed variant (`run_bidir_rescue.py
--num-seeds 5`): for each unsolved puzzle take the top-5 distinct
non-perfect seeds, do bidir-rescue (K=100 random-perm samples
in-painting) on each. Different seeds → different fixed scaffolds →
different generation contexts.

Results:
- Same 13/20 solved (no improvement).
- Per-puzzle max_jaccard barely budged: puzzle 0 went 0.956→0.977,
  puzzle 19 went 0.871→0.902, puzzles 3/4/5/12/17 unchanged.
- Total candidates per unsolved puzzle: 5 seeds × K=100 = 500
  (vs 100 single-seed, vs 256 enumeration). Still no perfects.

Mechanistic confirmation: across 7 unsolved puzzles, neither (a)
4^k local enumeration, (b) single-seed model-guided in-painting,
(c) multi-seed model-guided in-painting can recover Jaccard=1.0.
The failures are structural, not local. Use this trio of negative
results in the discussion to justify *why* rescue is one-sided.

### Future work for bidir rescue

Local 4^k mutation and model-guided in-painting at diff_pos both hit
the same structural ceiling on our seeds. More aggressive options
that *could* break it but are out of scope for this paper:

- **Multi-seed bidir-rescue**: rescue from top-N non-perfect seeds (not
  just top-1) so different fixed scaffolds give the model different
  contexts. Requires multiple K=100 generations per puzzle.
- **Iterated refinement**: rescue → re-evaluate diff_pos → rescue
  again. Convergence-style local search.
- **Ensemble of decoding orders**: rather than picking one perm per
  rescue sample, sweep across structurally-aware orders
  (paired-first, loop-outward) within each rescue attempt.
- **Larger compute budget**: K-sweep on unsolved puzzles to confirm
  whether the structural-ceiling explanation is real or whether more
  random-perm samples would eventually find perfects.

All of these are realistic-but-deferred. The paper's claim is
load-bearing already without them: per-sample reliability win, tied
coverage at matched diversity, mechanistic understanding of *why*
rescue is one-sided.
